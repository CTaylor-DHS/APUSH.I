AP U.S. History, Part One (1492-1865): Indigenous America to the Civil War

Course Description:
This course is a college-level introduction to American History from the Age of Exploration through the Civil War. We will focus on the emergence of the United States as a nation and the formation of a distinct American identity. The topics covered will address the economic, cultural, social, technological, militaristic, religious, racial, and ideological development of America during its formative years. Particular attention will be given to the differing perspectives, identities, and backgrounds that contributed to or were shaped by the American experience.

Learning Objectives:
1. Develop Historical Arguments (DHA) 
   1. Construct defensible, evidence-based written arguments that address complex historical questions.
2. Analyze Primary and Secondary Sources (APS) 
   1. Evaluate the historical context, audience, purpose, and point of view of a variety of primary and secondary sources.
3. Explain Historical Causation (EHC)
   1. Identify and analyze short and long term causes and effects of major developments from 1492 to 1865.
4. Analyze Continuity and Change Over Time (CCT)
   1. Trace how political, economic, social, cultural, and geographical systems evolved across historical periods.
5. Compare Historical Developments (CHD)
   1. Compare and contrast how different groups such as Native Americas, European Settlers, enslaved Africans, women, reformers, and political leaders experienced and shaped early America.
6. Evaluate Competing Interpretations (ECI)
   1. Assess differing perspectives and interpretations regarding major events such as colonization, the American Revolution, Westward expansion, slavery, and the Civil War.

Student Outcomes:
1. Write Document-Based Questions (DBQs) and Long Essay Questions (LEQs) that meet AP rubric standards.
2. Demonstrate the ability to analyze information across multiple historical periods.
3. Explain how race, geography, gender, class, and culture shaped competing social and political systems in early America.
4. Identify key turning points between 1492 and 1865 and justify their historical significance.
5. Support claims with specific and relevant primary and secondary source evidence.
6. Analyze how conflicts over power, labor systems, territorial expansion, and citizenship contributed to the outbreak of the Civil War.

Capstone: The capstone project serves as the cumulative assessment for U.S. History Part I (1492-1865) and is designed to measure your ability to synthesize and analyze historical developments across an extended period of time. You will complete a structured comparative analysis examining how two different groups experienced and shaped the development of the United States prior to 1865. In a multi-paragraph analytical essay, you will develop a defensible thesis, analyze change over time, and incorporate at least six primary sources. In addition, you will create a visual timeline identifying key turning points that shaped the relationship between your selected groups and deliver a brief presentation summarizing and defending your conclusions. This project emphasizes historical argumentation, evidence-based writing, and public speaking skills aligned with AP-level expectations while remaining appropriate for tenth-grade students.
Tests: In accordance with the standards set by Discovery High School, there will be Periodic Assessments in October, January, March, and May (this might change). These assessments will test your acquisition of content knowledge and source-reading skills through multiple-choice questions and written responses, such as extended paragraph responses, ID statements, or an alternative written component. The act of “doing history”, the ultimate goal of this course, requires a degree of familiarity with historical contexts and materials and so it is your responsibility to ensure that you are retaining and familiarizing yourself with the material. Consult with Mr. Taylor if you need assistance regarding the retention of information and together we will design a comprehensive plan tailored to your needs.

Document-Based Questions (DBQs): Document-Based Questions require you to construct a clear and defensible historical argument using a set of primary and secondary source documents. The DBQs will assess your ability to analyze historical sources and use them as evidence to support an argument. In each DBQ, you must develop a strong thesis, situate the topic within its broader historical context, and use the majority of the provided documents to support your claims. Successful responses demonstrate not only accurate knowledge but also complex historical reasoning and an ability to think critically about multiple perspectives.

Long Essay Questions (LEQs): Long Essay Questions require you to develop a clear, evidence-based historical argument using your own knowledge of U.S. history without the support of provided documents. The LEQs will assess your ability to apply historical reasoning skills while constructing a well-supported argument. Strong responses demonstrate organized reasoning, historical accuracy, and an understanding of the broader significance of the topic.

Citation Guidelines:
When referencing or citing sources, whether primary or secondary, you must adhere to the following citation guidelines. Stating “In Document 1, we see that…” is not citing a source. Citing a source requires: the title, the author, and year of publication. Therefore, the non-citation of “In Document 1…” becomes “In Thomas Paine’s pamphlet, Common Sense (1776), we see that…”. The reason for doing this is so that anyone is able to understand your writing and know which sources you are using to support your argument as opposed to the ambiguous, mysterious, and obscure  “Document 1”. Additionally, this also serves to highlight how the different documents we read in this course come from people, and it is the study of people that drives (most) historical inquiry.
